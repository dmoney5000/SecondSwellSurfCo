How to Choose a Beginner Surfboard (Without Wasting $700)

Second Swell Surf Co. · Cocoa Beach, FL · Updated June 2026

The most expensive mistake in surfing is not a broken board — it is the wrong first board. Every month somebody books an appointment at our garage to sell a barely-used shortboard they bought new, lost money on, and never learned to surf. This guide is the conversation we have at the rack, so you can skip that chapter entirely.

The one rule: volume is your friend

Volume — measured in liters — is how much board is floating you. More volume means easier paddling, earlier wave-catching, and a stable platform to stand on. That is everything when you are learning.

The rough math we use: a beginner wants volume around 80–100% of their body weight in kilograms. A 170 lb (77 kg) beginner should be on roughly 60–75 liters.

For reference, the pro-style shortboards you see in surf videos run 25–30 liters. That gap is exactly why beginners on shortboards do not catch waves.

Length, width, and shape

For most adults learning at Cocoa Beach, that volume math lands you on a board between 7'6" and 9'0" — a funboard, mid-length, or longboard. Wider is more stable (21"+ is your zone), and a rounded nose gives you forgiving real estate up front. Skip anything described as "performance," "step-up," or with a pointy nose. You can size down later; you cannot enjoy surfing on a board you cannot paddle.

Construction: soft top, epoxy, or PU?

Soft tops are forgiving when the board hits you (it will) and shrug off rocks and drops — the right call for your first months.

Epoxy hard boards are durable, buoyant, and a great second step once you are standing consistently.

Traditional polyester (PU) boards feel the best underfoot but ding the easiest. That is fine, since ding repair is cheap when it is done early.

The used-board math: a new beginner board runs $500–$800 and loses a third of its value the day it gets wet. A leak-checked used board does the identical job for $150–$350 — and when you improve in six months, you can trade it back in toward your next shape. That is the whole reason our used rack exists.

Red flags when buying used anywhere

Buying from a marketplace stranger? Check for four things, and walk away if you spot any of them:

Brown or yellow patches around dings (that is waterlog). Soft spots when you press the deck (delamination). Repairs that were painted over rather than properly glassed. And a board that feels heavy for its size.

Every board on our rack has been leak-checked, weighed, and graded specifically so you do not have to play that game — every past repair is listed right on the tag.
