Surfboard Restoration: Bringing an Old Board Back from the Dead

Second Swell Surf Co. · Cocoa Beach, FL · Updated June 2026

Every few weeks somebody texts us photos of a board pulled out of a garage rafter, a storage unit, or a late relative’s shed. Yellowed glass, brown waterlogged patches, a fin box full of spider webs — and the same question: can this be saved? Usually, yes. Whether it should be is a different question, and answering it honestly is the whole point of this guide.

What surfboard restoration actually means

Restoration is not a big ding repair. A ding repair fixes one wound; a restoration rebuilds the whole patient. A full restoration at our Cocoa Beach garage typically involves drying out the blank (sometimes for weeks), cutting out and replacing rotten foam, re-glassing damaged sections with cloth matched to the original schedule, rebuilding crushed noses and tails to the original template, replacing fin boxes and leash plugs, addressing delamination, and finishing with a wet-sanded gloss coat that brings the color back.

The restoration process, step by step

1. Assessment and drying

We weigh the board first. Foam has a known weight; everything over that is water, and water has to leave before resin goes anywhere near the board. Open dings get exposed and the board sits in a warm, dry space — rushing this step is how bad restorations fail a year later.

2. Structural surgery

Brown, mushy, or separated foam gets cut out and replaced with new blank foam, shaped to match the original curves. Buckles and stress fractures get opened up, reinforced, and re-glassed rather than smeared over.

3. Glasswork and color

New fiberglass is laid to match the board’s original weight and feel. On vintage boards we match resin tints and pigments so repairs disappear into the original color work — this is the slowest, most skilled part of the job, and it is the difference between a restoration and a cover-up.

4. Finish

A new hot coat, then gloss, then a wet-sand and polish. A properly finished restoration should make you squint to find where the work was done.

What it costs: small restorations start around $250–$400; full vintage longboard restorations commonly run $500–$900 depending on damage. We quote every project in writing after assessment — see our repair pricing for where smaller jobs start.

When restoration is worth it — and when it is not

Worth it: vintage and collectible boards, boards by respected shapers, and sentimental boards — dad’s longboard, your first board, the one with the story. Not worth it: a waterlogged 2014 pop-out that cost $300 new. For those, we will say so before you spend a dollar, and point you at our used rack or offer trade credit instead. Some boards deserve resin; some deserve a place on the wall. We will tell you which is which.
