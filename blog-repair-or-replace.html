Is Your Surfboard Worth Repairing? The Honest Math

Second Swell Surf Co. · Cocoa Beach, FL · Updated June 2026

Strange thing for a repair shop to publish, but here it is: some boards are not worth fixing, and we would rather tell you that for free than charge you to learn it. This is the framework we run on every board that comes through the garage.

The 50% rule

Start with what your board is honestly worth today — not what you paid, what a buyer would pay. If the repair quote exceeds roughly half of that number, think hard. A $120 rail repair on a $400 board is an obvious yes. A $250 buckle repair on a board worth $300 healthy is usually a no — unless the board clears one of the exceptions below.

Always repair

Fresh, small damage on a board you like: dings, cracks, leash plugs, fin boxes. These run $45–$150 at our bench, preserve the board’s full value, and — critically — stop water from turning a small problem into a structural one. The cheapest repair you will ever buy is the one you do not postpone.

Usually repair

Buckles and clean snaps on quality boards. A properly rebuilt snap restores most of the board’s strength for $200–$350, which beats replacing a $600+ board every time. Also in this bucket: anything by a respected shaper, anything custom, and anything you would be sad about in a year.

Usually replace

Heavy waterlog (the board feels like it gained a sibling), widespread delamination on a cheap blank, or a third major repair on a board that was entry-level to begin with. Here the smart play is different: we will quote the repair, but we will also offer trade credit on the damaged board and walk you to the used rack. Often you come out ahead — and your old board becomes our restoration practice or parts.

Cannot decide? Text photos to (321) 555-0199. We will reply the same day with one of three words: repair, trade, or retire — and the numbers behind each. No appointment needed until you are ready to drop off.

The exception that overrules all math

Sentiment. If it is your first board, your dad’s board, or the board from the trip you still talk about — the 50% rule does not apply and you should read our restoration guide instead. Some boards are worth fixing because of what they are, not what they are worth.
